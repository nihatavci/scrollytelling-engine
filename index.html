Act I · The Question

The Question That Haunted Demographers

In 1975, economist Samuel H. Preston published a paper that would reshape how we think about the relationship between wealth and survival. Examining data from three distinct decades of the 20th century, he found a striking pattern: richer nations lived longer. But the relationship wasn't linear. It curved.

The first few thousand dollars of national income per person bought enormous gains in life expectancy — clean water, basic medicine, childhood vaccines. But beyond a threshold, the curve flattened. Additional wealth purchased progressively fewer years. This concave relationship, now called the Preston Curve, raised a question that still haunts global policy: if money alone doesn't buy longevity, what does?

The correlation between national income and life expectancy reaches 0.90 in modern datasets — strikingly high. Yet it masks enormous variation. Some countries far outperform their wealth would predict; others fail spectacularly despite abundant resources. The curve, it turns out, is just the beginning of the story.

Act II · The Twist

The Upward Shift

"At any given level of income, a country in 1960 could expect to live much longer than a country at that same income level in 1930. Something beyond money was extending lives."

What Preston discovered next was even more remarkable. The entire curve was shifting upward over time. Countries in the 1960s lived longer than countries at the same income level in the 1930s. Something beyond GDP was extending lives — vaccines, antibiotics, clean water infrastructure, oral rehydration therapy. Preston called this the "exogenous upward shift."

Hans Rosling later brought this "Great Convergence" to public attention through his famous Gapminder visualizations, showing how nations migrated from the "poor and sick" corner toward "rich and healthy." But not all at the same pace. And not always because of wealth. A scholarly rivalry emerged: was it income or education that truly extended lives?

Wolfgang Lutz and Endale Kebede argued that educational attainment is the more powerful predictor. Education, they showed, improves health through cognitive reserve, better health literacy, and increased agency within complex medical systems. In developing countries, female education alone is a primary driver of child survival.

Act III · Beyond the Curve

The Countries That Defy the Data

"Costa Rica achieves American life expectancy at one-fifth the cost. Russia, despite moderate wealth, sits years below where the curve predicts."

The most compelling evidence that money alone doesn't buy years comes from the outliers. Costa Rica, Cuba, and the Indian state of Kerala have achieved life expectancies rivaling wealthy nations despite having a fraction of their income. Their formula: universal primary healthcare, high literacy, and strong public health systems — what researchers call "Good Health at Low Cost."

On the flip side, Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett's research revealed that in wealthy nations, it's not absolute income but inequality that kills. In equal societies like Japan and Sweden — where the rich are 3.5 times wealthier than the poor — health outcomes are consistently better. In the US and UK, where the gap reaches 7 to 8 times, chronic psychosocial stress erodes years of life. The Preston Curve, it turns out, has a hidden dimension: distribution.

The Resolution

Does money buy years? After 50 years of inquiry, the answer is nuanced. Money facilitates years, but it is not the engine of survival. The engine is what Preston called the "Upward Shift" — the relentless progress of human knowledge, education, and technology.

For the developing world, the most efficient path to longevity is not GDP growth but reaching the health technological frontier through specific interventions: vaccines, clean water, maternal care, literacy. For the wealthy world, the frontier has shifted to inequality, mental health, and the social determinants that no amount of national income can fix on its own.

The COVID-19 pandemic confirmed this. Between 2019 and 2021, global life expectancy dropped 1.8 years — its largest fall in recent history. The drop was not uniform: it was a stress test that revealed which societies had built resilience beyond wealth. By 2023, life expectancy rebounded to 73.3 years, driven largely by vaccines — another upward shift.

"In the 1975 Preston Curve, the story was about the wealth of nations. In 2025, it's about the resilience of societies and the plasticity of the human mind. We no longer buy years with gold; we cultivate them with knowledge."
